Overview

Learning Buddies Network (LBN) is a literacy and education nonprofit based in Vancouver that connects children with learning challenges to trained volunteer tutors. The organization serves families who rely on community support to access educational resources that would otherwise be out of reach.

During my internship, I was responsible for creating two promotional videos to introduce LBN to new audiences — families seeking support, volunteers considering joining the program, and community partners exploring collaboration. The videos needed to feel warm, trustworthy, and accessible: communicating the organization's mission without feeling corporate or clinical.

Creative Approach

The central challenge was representing a human, community-focused organization through video — without access to a large production budget or a professional film crew. The approach had to feel authentic rather than polished, and personal rather than promotional.

Each video was planned around a specific audience and a specific question that audience would have: families want to know "will this actually help my child?", while volunteers and partners want to know "what does this organization actually do and why does it matter?" The scripts and visual direction were shaped around answering those questions as directly and honestly as possible.

  • Warmth as a design requirement

    For a nonprofit working with children and families, tone is not optional — it is the message. Every creative decision, from music selection to pacing to visual framing, was evaluated against whether it reinforced a sense of safety and community rather than institutional distance.

  • Mission clarity in under two minutes

    Nonprofit promotional video often tries to say too much. Both scripts were disciplined to one core message each — who we are and what we do, told through a single clear narrative thread rather than a comprehensive program overview.

  • Audience-specific framing

    The two videos serve different audiences and answer different questions. Rather than producing one general video that tries to speak to everyone, the two-video approach let each piece speak directly and specifically — which makes both more effective than a single catch-all version would have been.

Produced Videos

Both videos were produced independently, covering scripting, visual direction, editing, and final delivery. The goal in both cases was a natural, community-first feel that reflects how LBN actually shows up for the families and children it serves.

Reflection

This internship reinforced that video storytelling for nonprofits is not primarily a production challenge — it is a communication design challenge. The hardest work is deciding what to say, who to say it to, and how to say it in a way that earns trust within the first twenty seconds.

Know who you're talking to

Producing two separate videos — one for families, one for partners — rather than one general video forced clarity about audience and message. Each video became stronger as a result. The constraint of one audience per video is a feature, not a limitation.

Trust is built through tone

For a community organization, the visual and audio tone of the video communicates values before the words do. Pacing, music, and visual warmth signal safety and authenticity to families — and those signals matter as much as any information the video conveys.

Working with purpose-driven constraints

Producing high-quality video without a large budget requires creative resourcefulness. This experience developed my ability to make strong editorial decisions that compensate for production limitations — choosing what to show, not just how to show it.
