Adobe Photoshop, Pixelmator Pro & Affinity Photo Comparison Video

Creating an Educational Comparison Video for Image Editing Tools

A practical, hands-on comparison that helps everyday users understand how Photoshop, Pixelmator Pro, and Affinity Photo differ in real workflows — covering usability, output quality, and pricing.

  • Overview
  • Content Focus
  • Production
  • Reflection

Structure

Group Work (3 members)

Tool

Photoshop · Pixelmator Pro · Affinity Photo · DaVinci Resolve

Timeline

3 Weeks

Responsibility

Comparison Content Writing · Video Editing · Voice Recording

Overview

This project is a group-created educational comparison video that introduces and contrasts Adobe Photoshop, Pixelmator Pro, and Affinity Photo. The video is aimed at casual users and beginners who are trying to choose between image editing tools — focusing on what each application actually feels like to use, rather than a feature-by-feature specification comparison.

The team worked together across content research, hands-on testing, script writing, voice recording, and final editing — with each member contributing across multiple stages rather than working in silos.

Content Focus

The comparison emphasizes real-world scenarios rather than theoretical feature lists. The goal was to answer the questions a typical user actually has before choosing a tool — not to exhaustively document every capability.

  • Hands-on task comparison

    Each tool was used to complete the same task — creating a meme and performing a basic photo edit — so viewers could see direct, like-for-like differences in how the interface behaves and how long each approach takes.

  • Pricing and value assessment

    The subscription vs. one-time purchase models across the three tools represent a real decision point for budget-conscious users. The video addresses this directly, helping viewers understand what they're actually paying for over one, two, and five year timeframes.

  • Accessibility for non-professional users

    The comparison was deliberately framed for casual and beginner users, not professionals who already know Photoshop. Technical features are explained in plain language — making the video useful to viewers who don't yet know what terms like "layer masking" or "RAW support" mean in practice.

Production & Editing

The video was edited in DaVinci Resolve, chosen for its precise timeline control and free availability — consistent with the video's theme of evaluating accessible tools. A professional microphone was used for all voice recording to ensure clear, broadcast-quality audio throughout.

  • Audio clarity as a priority

    Poor audio quality in comparison videos breaks trust — if the production feels amateur, viewers question whether the comparisons were conducted carefully. Investing in clean voice recording was a deliberate choice to maintain credibility throughout.

  • Pacing for educational content

    Educational videos require longer dwell time on key points than entertainment content. Cuts were timed to give viewers a moment to absorb what was just demonstrated before the narration moved on — preventing the cognitive overload that causes viewers to stop or rewind.

Reflection

This project reinforced that educational video requires a different approach to pacing and structure than promotional content. The viewer needs time to understand, not just to be impressed — which changes how information is sequenced, how fast the narration moves, and how much repetition is appropriate.

Know your audience's baseline

Writing for beginners meant constantly checking whether a term or concept had been explained before using it. This produced a more inclusive video, but required discipline — the temptation to assume shared knowledge is constant when you already know the tools well.

Demonstrate, don't describe

The most effective moments in the video are the side-by-side task demonstrations. Showing the tools doing the same thing is more persuasive than describing the difference in words — it lets viewers form their own judgment from what they see.

Group editing requires shared standards

With three team members contributing content and editing, establishing shared standards for pacing, tone, and terminology early prevented inconsistencies in the final cut. Alignment before production is faster than reconciliation after.
