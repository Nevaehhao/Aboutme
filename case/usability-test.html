Usability Test

Evaluating the Usability & Information Architecture of an Academic Portal

Assessing the uoZone platform to improve findability, navigation clarity, and user confidence for students managing complex academic workflows.

Overview

uoZone is a critical academic platform, but students struggle to find key information due to unclear navigation and poor information structure, leading to confusion, inefficiency, and reliance on external search. These issues increase task completion time, reduce user confidence, and force users to rely on external tools.

Research Objectives

To identify and resolve these usability issues, our research evaluated user interactions across 6 key tasks, measuring:

Effectiveness

Task completion rates and error recovery.

Efficiency

Time on task, click paths, and navigation detours.

Satisfaction

Perceived usability and emotional response, measured via the System Usability Scale (SUS)

Methodology

We recruited 5 uOttawa students (Undergrad, Grad, and PhD) using a targeted screener to ensure high-quality, unbiased data.

1. Think-Aloud Protocol

Participants were asked to verbalize their thought process, expectations, and moments of confusion in real-time.

2. Neutral Facilitation

To capture authentic user behavior, we avoided guiding the participants, letting them fail or find workarounds naturally to expose true system flaws.

We designed 6 scenario-based tasks focusing on critical academic and administrative goals, chosen for their high usage frequency.

Research & Data Analysis

We applied multiple usability testing methods and data analyses to uncover where and why uOttawa students experience friction, confusion, or drop off when navigating the uoZone platform.

We analyzed performance data from Loop11, including task success/abandonment rates, time-on-task, and page views, to objectively measure system efficiency and effectiveness.

By tracking users' actual navigation flows against expected paths, we identified severe mental model mismatches and navigation detours.

Through the Think-Aloud protocol and post-task System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaires, we synthesized behavioral observations, emotional responses, and user quotes.

We utilized first-click heatmaps to visualize user attention and initial decision-making.

Key Findings & Insights

Severe Findability Issues & External Search Reliance

Critical features are deeply hidden, causing massive task abandonment and forcing users to rely on external search engines (e.g., Google).
The "Applications" page is severely overloaded with similar icons. Finding non-frequent tools like "Volunteer Registration" or "Co-Curricular Record (CCR)" creates extreme cognitive overload.
  • 80% Abandonment Rate: 4 out of 5 users completely abandoned the volunteer registration task, rating it "Extremely difficult".
  • High Interaction Cost: Users navigated up to 17 pages looking for their CCR, with only a 20% success rate. 40% of users switched to Google out of frustration.
  • User Quotes: "I need to look through a thousand buttons... I feel clueless." / "I probably just go Google."

IA Mismatch with Mental Models

The system's categorization contradicts students' natural intuition, resulting in significant navigation detours.
Students naturally expect to find "Degree Progress" within the "Course Enrollment" flow, but the system separates them. Meanwhile, the actual shortcut suffers from severe banner blindness.
  • Behavioral Deviation: 80% (4/5) of users intuitively clicked "Enroll" first to check their degree progress.
  • Banner Blindness: 0/5 users noticed the direct "My Academic Requirements" shortcut on the Applications page.
  • Complex Click Paths: Users took 7+ steps in maze-like paths (e.g., Applications > Enroll > Plan > Enroll > My Class Schedule > My Academics...).

Ambiguous Terminology & Poor System Feedback

Academic jargon and unclear system status feedback drastically reduce user confidence and increase anxiety.
First-year students struggle with obscure terms like "Advisement Report" or abbreviations like "CCR". Additionally, ambiguous empty states and missing loading spinners make users think the system is broken.
  • Unclear Empty States: When no exams were scheduled, the system simply showed "An exam schedule is not available for this term", leaving users unsure if they navigated to the wrong page.
  • Missing Feedback: Clicking "View Report" for transcripts triggered no loading animation. Users repeatedly clicked the button, assuming the site froze.
  • User Quote: "Probably if they can tell me I don't have any exams, it will be better as well."

Reflection

Data Discrepancy Analysis

When using Loop11 for usability testing, system issues arose affecting participants' ability to complete the tests. This meant the test results couldn't be used directly; video recordings were required. Instead of relying on automatically generated reports, I manually reviewed the recordings, recalculated metrics, and ensured data integrity.

Actions Over Opinions

This project reinforced a core UX principle: what users do matters more than what they say. Findings were grounded in actual click path deviations and task abandonment rates rather than simply soliciting opinions — producing evidence that stakeholders cannot easily dismiss.
