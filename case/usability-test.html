Usability Test

Evaluating the Usability & Information Architecture of an Academic Portal

A structured usability study using Loop11 to identify navigation, findability, and labeling issues in a university academic portal — generating data-backed recommendations that improve the user experience for students and staff.

Overview

Academic portals serve as central hubs for students and faculty — managing course registration, grade access, financial records, and institutional resources. When these systems are difficult to navigate, the cost is real: missed deadlines, repeated support requests, and user frustration that erodes trust in the institution's digital services.

This project evaluated the usability and information architecture of an academic portal through a structured remote usability study using Loop11. As team lead and primary moderator, I designed the test plan, defined the task set, facilitated moderated sessions, and led the synthesis of quantitative and qualitative findings into five categories of actionable recommendations.

Key problems identified going into the evaluation:

  • Unclear navigation structure

    The portal's top-level navigation did not map to users' mental models of what an academic portal contains — categories were ambiguously labeled and grouped by system logic rather than user tasks.

  • Low findability for common tasks

    Core tasks — finding a transcript, locating a course syllabus, accessing financial aid — required more steps than users expected, leading to high error rates and backtracking on first attempts.

  • Inconsistent labeling

    Menu items and page titles used institutional terminology that new users and international students were unfamiliar with, creating friction at every entry point.

  • Weak visual hierarchy

    Important actions and links lacked visual distinction from secondary content — users frequently scanned past target elements without recognizing them as actionable.

  • Limited system feedback

    The portal provided minimal confirmation or error messaging, leaving users uncertain whether actions had been completed — particularly on form submissions and registration flows.

Methodology

We used Loop11, a remote unmoderated and moderated usability testing platform, to capture both behavioral data and qualitative feedback from real portal users. The study combined task-based testing with System Usability Scale (SUS) scoring and post-task interview questions to build a complete picture of the user experience.

  • Remote moderated & unmoderated sessions

    Participants completed tasks independently through Loop11's unmoderated flow, while a subset of sessions were moderated via screen share to capture think-aloud commentary. This hybrid approach balanced coverage with depth.

  • Six-task scenario design

    Tasks were designed to cover the portal's core user journeys — from registration and grade access to financial aid navigation. Each task had a clear, observable success condition that Loop11 tracked automatically.

  • SUS scoring for baseline measurement

    All participants completed a System Usability Scale questionnaire after each session, providing a standardized overall score alongside task-level data — making findings comparable across participant groups and future iterations.

Research & Data Analysis

Loop11 captured task completion rates, time-on-task, and click paths automatically. These quantitative signals were combined with SUS scores and moderation session transcripts to identify where users struggled, why they struggled, and how consistently those issues appeared across participants.

First-click analysis and click path replay were particularly valuable — they revealed not only where users ended up, but what they tried first, exposing the gap between the portal's structure and users' initial mental models.

  • Quantitative signals from Loop11

    Task completion rates, success paths, and time-on-task data from Loop11 identified which tasks had the highest error rates. Tasks related to financial aid and academic records showed the lowest completion rates and the most backtracking.

  • Click path analysis

    Click path replays surfaced recurring wrong-turn patterns — users consistently navigating into the wrong section before finding the correct path. These patterns indicated structural and labeling problems rather than one-off user errors.

  • First-click heatmaps

    First-click heatmaps showed where users looked first for each task. Mismatches between first-click behavior and the actual location of target content confirmed that navigation labels were not matching user expectations.

Key Findings & Insights

Analysis across all sessions produced five recurring issue categories — each supported by quantitative data from Loop11 and qualitative observations from moderated sessions. These findings formed the direct basis for the recommendation framework.

  • Navigation & Information Architecture

    The portal's top-level navigation structure reflected administrative categorization rather than user task flows. Users looking for academic records, financial information, or course tools all started in the wrong section before finding the correct path — a consistent signal of IA misalignment.

  • Findability

    High-priority content — transcripts, financial aid, grade history — was buried multiple levels deep, with no shortcuts or surface-level signposting. Users expected these items to be prominent and were surprised by how many clicks were required to reach them.

  • Labeling

    Menu labels and section titles used institutional jargon that created friction for new users. Terms like "Enrolment Services" and "Student Central" were not immediately legible — participants guessed at their meaning rather than navigating with confidence.

  • Visual Hierarchy

    Key links and action items did not stand out visually from surrounding content. Users repeatedly scanned past their target without registering it — a problem compounded by dense page layouts with low visual differentiation between primary and secondary content.

  • Feedback & System Status

    The portal provided minimal feedback after user actions — no confirmation messages on form submission, no clear error states, and no indication of progress through multi-step processes. Users frequently questioned whether their actions had been registered.

Recommendation

Each recommendation category addresses one of the five identified issue areas, paired with specific before/after examples to make the proposed changes concrete and implementable for the portal's development team.

1. Navigation & Information Architecture

Restructure the top-level navigation around user tasks and mental models rather than administrative categories. Group items by what users are trying to accomplish — "My Courses," "My Finances," "My Records" — rather than by which department owns the content. Introduce a persistent quick-access bar for the portal's five most-used features.

2. Findability

Surface the portal's most frequently accessed content at the top level. Transcripts, financial aid summaries, and course schedules should be reachable in one or two clicks from the homepage. Add a prominent search function with autocomplete to serve users who cannot locate items through browsing.

3. Labeling

Replace institutional terminology with plain, task-oriented language throughout the navigation and page headings. Conduct a labeling audit using card sorting or tree testing to validate that proposed labels match users' vocabulary before implementation — particularly for sections that serve international students.

4. Visual Hierarchy

Apply stronger visual differentiation between primary actions and secondary content. Use size, weight, and color contrast to make high-priority links and CTAs visually distinct at a glance — reducing the cognitive work of scanning dense pages and ensuring users find their target on the first pass.

5. Feedback & System Status

Add clear confirmation states, error messages, and progress indicators throughout all transactional flows. Users should always know whether their action succeeded, failed, or is still in progress — following Nielsen's heuristic of system visibility. This is especially critical for registration, financial aid, and grade-change requests.

Reflection

Leading this usability study showed me how structured research methods create shared evidence — findings that a development team or stakeholder cannot easily dismiss because they come from actual user behavior, not designer opinion.

Data reduces opinion

Loop11's quantitative output — task completion rates, click paths, time-on-task — gave every recommendation a measurable basis. When stakeholders push back on UX changes, data from real users shifts the conversation from "I think" to "we observed."

Moderation reveals the why

Unmoderated sessions showed where users struggled; moderated sessions explained why. Think-aloud commentary during moderation surfaced labeling confusion and false assumptions that click data alone would never have revealed — confirming that mixed methods produce richer findings than either approach alone.

Leading research as a team sport

Coordinating five people across study design, recruitment, moderation, and synthesis required clear role boundaries and shared documentation. The most valuable process decisions — defining task success criteria upfront, agreeing on a synthesis framework — were the ones made before any participant joined the study.
